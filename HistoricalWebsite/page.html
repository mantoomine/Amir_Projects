First Story

He has a strange birthday. His mother is a Turanian girl who accidentally (and perhaps miraculously) enters the court of Kavus. He is the ultimate youth of beauty, bravery, piety, kindness and forgiveness. Siavash does not give in to sin, the fire testifies to his purity, and he is killed oppressed by the jealousy and malice of evil people. This Iranian myth has an undeniable resemblance to the three characters of the Abrahamic religions. Like Joseph, he escapes from the trap of sin that a cunning woman throws at him, because Abraham, by the grace of God, does not set him on fire, and because John is killed oppressed and his murderers pay a heavy price for this crime.

One day, Tus, along with Goodarz, Gio and Chandsavar, go hunting in Nakhjirgah and see a beautiful girl there. The girl says that she is a relative of Gersius (brother of Afrasiab, king of Turan) and fled to Iran because her father was angry and wanted to kill her. Tus and Gio both like their daughter and they intend to marry her and they get involved in this issue. They take the case to Kikavous, the king of Iran, but Kikavous falls in love with his daughter and marries her. The result of this connection is a handsome boy who, when he is born, is filled with joy and gives him the name of Siavakhsh. But astrologers see his astrology very disturbing and warn Kavous. The Shah entrusts Siavash to Rustam for upbringing and education. Rustam takes him to Zabulistan for upbringing. . .

During the reign of Manouchehr, Sam Nariman, the commander and hero of Iran, ruled Zabulistan. She has a white-haired son. Sam, who considers this congenital defect of his child a disgrace to himself; He orders the child to be left in the Alborz mountains to be preyed upon by birds and predators. But a bird named Simorgh hears the baby crying and has mercy on him and raises him with his children. After years, Sam finds his son dreaming that he has become beautiful, strong and brave.
